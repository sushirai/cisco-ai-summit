"I swore I'd never give Codex full control of my computer. That lasted about two hours."

01 AI coding agents crossed a threshold

Altman opened with a personal confession: Codex, OpenAI's coding agent, gave him his biggest "update" on AI in a long time — a second ChatGPT moment. He anchored this immediately in Cisco's own experience, noting G2's team would have 100% of AI Defense code written by Codex within weeks. Not a demo — a proof point from the interviewer's own company.

02 Endgame: full AI companies

From there he escalated deliberately. He moved from coding agents to "full AI companies" — AI that doesn't just write software but builds a business around it. Called it "the upper limit I can currently think about," his way of saying the ceiling keeps moving.

03 The two-hour capitulation

Then came a fascinatingly honest admission. He installed Codex, swore he'd never give it full control — lasted about two hours before caving because it was too useful. Now runs two laptops because he couldn't resist giving AI full access to his sessions. This personal anecdote conveyed more about agentic AI's power than any benchmark.

Key Insight

The real blockers aren't about model intelligence. They're about the fact that your existing software stack isn't built for a world where agents and humans share the same tools.

04 Your software stack will break

He laid out what he sees as the real blockers — and none were about model intelligence. First: the security and data access paradigm hasn't been invented yet. Second: software needs rewriting for dual human-AI use. He illustrated this with a wonderfully mundane example — his AI agent reads Slack threads but marks everything as read, breaking his workflow. A "silly" example aimed directly at every CIO in the room: your software stack isn't built for a world where agents and humans share the same tools.

05 The capability overhang

The deepest concept was the "capability overhang" — the gap between what AI can do and what organizations actually use it for. He said this gap feels bigger now than it was right before ChatGPT launched, even though everyone's already using AI. His advice was blunt: companies that can't quickly adopt "AI co-workers" will be at a huge disadvantage, and getting there requires thinking of AI as a teammate, not a tool.

"The capability overhang — the gap between what AI can do and what people are doing with it — is the largest it's ever been."

— Sam Altman, on enterprise adoption
06 10x better by end of 2026

He closed on scale, framing AI demand like electricity — infinite at the right price. Predicted a 10x improvement in models by end of 2026. When pressed on imagination limits, offered a vision from billions of humanoid robots to Von Neumann probes in space — then admitted "beyond that, I got no idea." A rare moment of genuine humility from someone who otherwise projected total conviction that AI is about to reshape everything.
